Global Profiles for Internal Displacement (GPID)

Who Are the World's Displaced?

Shedding light on the socio-economic profiles of impacted communities

Internal displacements due to conflict and disasters are a major driver of human mobility worldwide. While the total numbers of internal displacements by cause, location, and time are well documented, global estimates of the demographic and socio-economic profiles of impacted communities have been missing. This lack of disaggregated data for variables such as age and sex, income, education, and health, land use and subsistence, has limited insights into of the diverse vulnerabilities and needs of displacement-impacted communities, which has complicated the work of decision makers who need to allocate resources efficiently.

IOM’s Global Profiles for Internal Displacement (GPID) dataset aims at bridging this knowledge gap. Combining global geolocated displacement data compiled by the Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre (IDMC) with high-resolution global maps of demographic and socio-economic variables, the dataset provides consistent estimates of key characteristics of populations in locations of internal displacement across the world.

The variables provided in the dataset include:

Demographic profile
  • Average age
  • Percentage of females (women and girls)
  • Percentage of males (men and boys)
  • Percentage of infants (younger than 1 year)
  • Percentage of children (younger than 18 years)
  • Percentage of elders (older than 65 years)
Socio-economic background
  • Average income
  • Average number of schooling years
  • Average life expectancy
Land use
  • Percentage of cropland in displacement area
  • Percentage of grazing area in displacement area
  • Percentage of urban area in displacement area

Methodology

GPID is based on a geolocated global dataset of internal displacement events caused by conflict, floods, storms, wildfires, earthquakes, droughts, and other disasters since 20181IDMC, 2024b. Each record contains the year of the event, geographical coordinates (usually at administrative level 1 (province, state, etc.) resolution), the cause of displacement, and the number of displacements. We combined these records with global gridded maps (usually at 0.083° resolution, i.e., approximately 10 km at the equator) of the following socio-economic and geographical variables: local age and sex structure2World Population Hub, n.d., per-capita gross national income (GNI), average life expectancy, and mean years of schooling,3Kummu et al., 2018 percentage of land covered by cropland, grazing land, and urban land,4Klein Goldewijk et al., 2017 and percentage of land managed and/or controlled by Indigenous Peoples.5Garnett et al., 2018

To make these data compatible with the displacement records, we aggregated all maps to administrative level 1 level, using the population-weighted average of the grid cells within each administrative unit,2World Population Hub, n.d. and using the weighted median for per-capita GNI and the weighted mean for all other variables. After assigning values to each displacement event, we computed the distribution of each variable across events for a given area (world, region, or country) and time (individual year or all years combined), weighing the value from each displacement by the number of displacements associated with the event.

Crucially, by the nature of the available data, our approach is limited to assessing the profiles of the general populations in locations in which internal displacements occurred, rather than directly analysing the populations that were displaced. While ground surveys conducted during displacement events provide valuable direct insights into those who move (https://dtm.iom.int/datasets), they lack the methodological consistency and global coverage needed for a comprehensive assessment. In the absence of such data, our estimates serve as a methodologically consistent, global-scale statistical baseline of the characteristics of populations impacted by internal displacement.
